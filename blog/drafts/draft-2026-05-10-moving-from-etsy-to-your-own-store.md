# Moving from Etsy to your own store: when to leave, when to stay

Every small maker I talk to hits the same wall around year three on Etsy. The store is doing real money, fees are taking a real bite, and the algorithm just changed again in a way that tanked impressions. The conversation always starts the same way: "Should I just move to my own site?"

The honest answer is: probably, but later than you think, and not the way most people do it.

This post walks through when leaving Etsy actually pays off, when staying is the smarter play, and what the migration looks like if you do pull the trigger. It is written for the kind of small Wabash Valley operation I see most often — someone selling $30K to $200K a year on Etsy, doing the work themselves or with a part-time helper, and getting tired of paying Etsy a tax on every sale they would have made anyway.

## What Etsy actually costs in 2026

Before the migration math makes sense, you have to add up what you are already paying Etsy. Most sellers underestimate this badly because the fees come out in pieces.

Listing fee: $0.20 per item, every four months. Forty active listings is $24 a year. Three hundred listings is $180 a year. Background noise for most sellers, but it counts.

Transaction fee: 6.5% of the total order, including shipping. This is the big one. On a $50 order with $8 shipping, Etsy takes $3.77 before payment processing.

Payment processing: 3% plus $0.25 per order in the US. On that same $50 order, another $1.75.

Offsite Ads: 12% to 15% of any order Etsy decides came from one of their ad placements. You cannot opt out once you cross $10K in sales for the year. This is the fee that makes long-time sellers angriest, because Etsy gets to claim the credit even when the customer was already searching for your brand by name.

Etsy Plus: $10 a month if you want it. Star Seller perks, ad credits, custom shop URL.

Add it up on a real number. A seller doing $80,000 a year on Etsy is paying somewhere in the range of $9,500 to $13,000 in Etsy fees, depending on how much Offsite Ads bites. That is twelve to sixteen percent of revenue going to a platform you do not own.

For comparison, a Shopify Basic store doing the same volume pays $39 a month for the platform, plus 2.9% plus $0.30 per transaction in payment processing. All in, around $2,800 to $3,200 a year. Same revenue, roughly $7,000 to $10,000 less in platform overhead.

That gap is what makes the migration tempting. But the gap is not the whole picture.

## What Etsy actually does for you

The thing you have to be honest about is what those fees buy. Etsy is not just a platform. It is a search engine, a buyer audience, and a trust layer. New buyers who have never heard of you find your products because Etsy puts them in front of buyers actively searching for handmade goods. That traffic does not exist on your own Shopify site on day one. You have to build it.

A typical Etsy shop's traffic mix is roughly 60 to 75 percent from Etsy search and Etsy's own promotional surfaces. Maybe 15 to 25 percent from social media you drive yourself. Maybe 5 to 15 percent from direct or repeat customers.

When you move to your own store, that 60 to 75 percent disappears overnight unless you have a real plan for replacing it. Which is exactly where most failed Etsy migrations die — the seller leaves Etsy, watches their revenue collapse from $7,000 a month to $1,500 a month, panics, and either crawls back or gives up.

## The honest test: should you actually leave?

Here are the questions that decide it.

**Are at least 30% of your Etsy orders from repeat customers or people who searched for your brand by name?** If yes, those customers will follow you. They are looking for *you*, not for "handmade leather wallet." If no, you are mostly running on Etsy's traffic, and you do not own your audience yet.

**Do you have an email list with at least 500 engaged subscribers?** This is the single best predictor of a successful migration. Email is the only marketing channel you fully own. If you cannot email 500 people on launch day and expect 100 of them to click through to your new store, you do not have a real audience yet.

**Do you have any non-Etsy traffic coming in?** Your own Instagram, TikTok, Pinterest, blog, anything. If 100% of your revenue depends on Etsy showing your products to strangers, leaving will hurt.

**Are you willing to spend three to six months in revenue dip while you rebuild traffic?** This is the part nobody warns sellers about. Even a clean migration with a strong brand and email list typically sees a 30 to 50 percent revenue drop in the first 90 days. It comes back, often higher than before, but you have to plan cash flow around it.

If you can answer yes to three of those four, migration probably makes sense. If you can only answer yes to one or two, stay on Etsy and spend a year fixing the underlying problem first — building email, building social presence, building brand recognition. Then migrate from a position of strength.

## The hybrid play most sellers should make first

Before you fully leave Etsy, run both for a year. Etsy stays open. You launch your own store on Shopify or BigCommerce in parallel. Every Etsy customer gets a thank-you card in the box with a discount code for ordering direct next time. Every social post drives to your own site, not your Etsy listing.

What this does: it lets you build your direct-to-consumer channel without giving up the discovery traffic Etsy provides. New customers find you on Etsy, then Etsy effectively becomes your customer acquisition channel and your own store becomes your retention channel.

Math on this changes the whole picture. If you can get 30% of repeat orders happening on your own site instead of Etsy, you are saving real money on those orders without giving up new customer flow. Once your own site is doing 50% of revenue, *then* you can have a real conversation about whether to close Etsy or keep it as an acquisition channel forever.

I have seen sellers run this hybrid for two or three years and never close Etsy. That is fine. Etsy does not have to be all-or-nothing.

## Where to actually go

If you decide to migrate (or run the hybrid), the two realistic options for most Etsy sellers are Shopify and BigCommerce. WooCommerce is theoretically cheaper but the maintenance burden is real and most makers do not want to be amateur sysadmins.

Shopify is the right answer for almost everyone moving from Etsy. The app ecosystem is enormous, the themes are good, the checkout converts well, and there is a Shopify-shaped solution for every operational problem you have. Start on Basic at $39 a month. Upgrade only when you hit a specific limit you can name.

BigCommerce is the right answer if you have a large catalog (over 500 SKUs), variant-heavy products, or you want to avoid Shopify's transaction fee on top of the standard payment processing rate. The platform is technically more capable per dollar than Shopify, but the app ecosystem is smaller and you will sometimes have to build things Shopify users get for free.

What you should probably not do: WooCommerce, Wix, or Squarespace. Each has its place but none of them are a good migration target from Etsy specifically. WooCommerce because of the maintenance burden, Wix and Squarespace because their e-commerce features are still not deep enough for a real product business.

## The migration playbook

If you are doing this yourself, here is the order of operations that actually works.

**Build the new site fully before you announce anything.** Do not soft-launch. Do not put up a half-finished store and tell customers about it. Get every product photographed, written, priced, and configured. Test checkout with a real card. Test mobile. Test shipping calculations. Order one of your own products from your own site and make sure the whole flow works.

**Set up email capture from day one.** Klaviyo if you can swing it ($0 free up to 250 contacts), Mailchimp or Constant Contact otherwise. Welcome series, abandoned cart, post-purchase. Get this running before you have a single customer.

**Move customers, not products.** Export your Etsy customer list (you can do this from Etsy's dashboard for any customer who consented to marketing). Import into Klaviyo. Send a real launch email — not a template, not a discount code blast, an actual letter explaining why you are launching your own site and what they get out of it. The discount can come in the next email.

**Keep Etsy open during the transition.** The hybrid model again. Do not close Etsy on launch day. You will need the cushion.

**Set up 301 redirects from your Etsy URLs.** This is one most people miss. If you have any backlinks to your Etsy listings (from your blog, press coverage, social bios), point them to the equivalent product on your new site. Your domain registrar or a free service can handle this for the public-facing links.

**Tell every customer in the box.** Order inserts in every Etsy shipment for the next 12 months saying "Order direct next time and save 10%" with a QR code to your new site. This is the highest-converting acquisition channel you will ever have, and it costs almost nothing.

## When the migration is straight-up wrong

A few cases where moving from Etsy to your own store does not pay off.

You sell under $20,000 a year. The math just does not work. The fees you save are not worth the lost discovery traffic plus the time and money to set up and maintain your own store. Stay on Etsy and focus on growing.

Your products genuinely depend on Etsy's discovery flow. If 95% of your traffic comes from Etsy search and you have no brand recognition, no email list, and no other channels, you are not ready to leave. Build those first.

You hate marketing. Running your own store means doing your own customer acquisition. If the appeal of Etsy is that they bring buyers to you, leaving Etsy means doing that yourself. Some sellers hate that work and would rather pay the fees forever. That is a legitimate choice.

You are about to retire or wind down. If your business has a five-year horizon, the cost of migration probably does not amortize. Stay on Etsy and milk it.

## Bottom line

Etsy fees are real and worth paying attention to. But the worst migration outcome is not paying high fees forever — it is leaving Etsy without a plan, watching revenue crater, and quitting. The fees Etsy charges are buying you something. Make sure you can replace it before you stop paying.

For most sellers in the $50K to $200K range, the right answer is the hybrid: open your own store, run both for at least a year, and let the channels balance out naturally. After 12 months you will have real data on what each channel is worth and you can make the close-Etsy decision with numbers instead of feelings.
