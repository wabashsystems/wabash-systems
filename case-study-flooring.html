The Challenge

Moving off a dead platform without losing the business.

The store was running on Magento 1 Enterprise — Adobe's most customizable ecommerce platform, and also the one they officially killed in June 2020. Enterprise licensees were getting security patches but no roadmap, no new features, and a shrinking pool of agencies willing to touch it.

Staying was not an option. But leaving M1 Enterprise is not like leaving a Shopify starter store.

Every meaningful feature in an M1 Enterprise store tends to be a custom module, a theme override, or a database-level modification written by whichever developer was on the project five years ago. Moving to BigCommerce — which is semi-open-source, API-first, and deliberately less flexible than raw Magento — means rebuilding those features inside platform constraints instead of inheriting freedom.

Getting wrong:

  • One hour of downtime at cutover would have cost more than four figures in lost sales and would have surfaced in every abandonment metric for a month.
  • A 10% drop in organic traffic would have meant rebuilding from a lower baseline — ad spend would have to compensate for months.
  • A botched data migration would have broken reorder patterns, loyalty history, and every back-office report stakeholders depend on.
  • A workaround that only half-works inside BigCommerce would have quietly eroded conversion for years before anyone pinned down the cause.
The Approach

Rebuild the custom, keep the invisible working.

Enterprise M1 stores are full of invisible infrastructure — scripts that run nightly, integrations that don't show up in the admin, reports that one person in accounting depends on. You cannot rebuild what you haven't mapped first. The first month of the engagement was discovery — not coding.

Only after we had a full map of every custom module, every integration, every cron job, and every downstream dependency did the real work start.

Phase 1
Discovery & mapping
Full audit of every custom module, cron, theme override, and integration. Identified which features would move to BigCommerce native, which needed apps, which needed custom work, and which could be retired.

Phase 2
Rebuild & parallel run
BigCommerce store stood up in parallel. Theme rebuilt to match the existing brand. Custom work included a shipping carrier API job to replace a Magento module. ERP kept separate — we built clean hooks rather than force a platform-level integration.
Months 2–7
Phase 3
Cutover & harden
Full 301 redirect map from every M1 URL to its BigCommerce equivalent. SEO preservation was non-negotiable. DNS cutover executed with rollback ready. Post-launch monitoring for a full business cycle before closing the engagement.
Months 8–9
The Outcome

Every metric that mattered held.

A good platform migration is invisible to customers and boring for stakeholders. No shipping delays. No checkout errors. No confused re-order emails from loyal customers asking where their account went. No traffic dip that shows up in the Monday report.

That was the outcome here. The store kept selling through the transition, the finance team kept running the same reports, and three years later the business is still on the platform we moved them to.

Why It Worked

The difference between a migration and a gamble.

Discovery first, code later
Most failed migrations skip or rush discovery. You can't rebuild what you haven't mapped. The first month was all interviews, audits, and inventory — no production code.
Parallel infrastructure, not big-bang
BigCommerce went live in parallel with Magento for months before cutover. Every integration was tested against real data before a single customer was routed to the new store.
A redirect map that wasn't an afterthought
Every M1 URL mapped to its BigCommerce equivalent before go-live, including legacy category paths and edge cases. SEO equity survived because it was protected deliberately, not hoped into existence.
